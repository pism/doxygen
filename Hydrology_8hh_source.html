49 //! \brief The PISM subglacial hydrology model interface.
50 /*!
51  This is a virtual base class.
52 
53  The purpose of this class and its derived classes is to provide
54  \code
55  subglacial_water_thickness()
56  subglacial_water_pressure()
57  till_water_thickness()
58  \endcode
59  These correspond to state variables \f$W\f$, \f$P\f$, and \f$W_{\text{till}}\f$
60  in [\ref BuelervanPeltDRAFT], though not all derived classes of Hydrology
61  have all of them as state variables.
62 
63  Additional modeled fields, for diagnostic purposes, are
64  \code
65  overburden_pressure(IceModelVec2S &result)
66  wall_melt(IceModelVec2S &result)
67  \endcode
68 
69  This interface is appropriate to subglacial hydrology models which track a
70  two-dimensional water layer with a well-defined thickness and pressure at each
71  map-plane location. The methods subglacial_water_thickness() and
72  subglacial_water_pressure() return amount and pressure of the *transportable*
73  water, that is, the subglacial water which moves along a modeled hydraulic
74  head gradient, in contrast to the water stored in the till.
75 
76  The transportable water moves through a subglacial morphology which is not
77  determined in this base class.
78 
79  The Hydrology models have separate, but potentially-coupled, water
80  which is held in local till storage. Thus the
81  transportable water (bwat) and till water (tillwat) thicknesses are different.
82  Published models with till storage include [\ref BBssasliding, \ref SchoofTill,
83  \ref TrufferEchelmeyerHarrison2001, \ref Tulaczyketal2000b, \ref vanderWeletal2013].
84 
85  The till water thickness is can be used, via the theory of
86  [\ref Tulaczyketal2000], to compute an effective pressure for the water in the
87  pore spaces of the till, which can then be used by the Mohr-Coulomb criterion
88  to provide a yield stress. Class MohrCoulombYieldStress does this
89  calculation. Here in Hydrology only the till water thickness tillwat is
90  computed.
91 
92  Hydrology is a timestepping component. Because of the
93  short physical timescales associated to liquid water moving under a glacier,
94  Hydrology (and derived) classes generally take many substeps in PISM's major
95  ice dynamics time steps. Thus when an update() method in a Hydrology
96  class is called it will advance its internal time to the new goal t+dt
97  using its own internal time steps.
98 
99  Generally Hydrology classes use the ice geometry, the basal melt
100  rate, and the basal sliding velocity in determining the evolution of the
101  hydrology state variables. Note that the basal melt rate is an
102  energy-conservation-derived field and the basal-sliding velocity is derived
103  from the solution of a stress balance. The basal melt rate and
104  sliding velocity fields therefore generally come from IceModel and
105  StressBalance, respectively.
106 
107  Additional, time-dependent and spatially-variable water input to the basal
108  layer, taken directly from a file, is possible too.
109 
110  Ice geometry and energy fields are normally treated as constant in time
111  during the update() call for the interval [t,t+dt]. Thus the coupling is
112  one-way during the update() call.
113 */
